Today’s Briefing — 2026-07-20

Analysis

Three days from the MPC and the most important new input landed over the weekend, and it cuts my way: the BER Inflation Expectations Survey shows average expectations across analysts, businesses and trade unions jumping from 3.6% to 4.4% for 2026, with 2027 at 4.2%, 2028 at 3.9% and the five-year at 4.1% (FNB weekly, reported by IOL/Business Report 19 Jul). That is the de-anchoring I asserted on 19 July when I wrote that expectations un-anchor cheaply and re-anchor expensively - now measured rather than argued, and the strongest evidence yet for the hike I called on 16 July. The same piece hands me a problem I should name rather than bury: the June CPI consensus has drifted DOWN toward 4.5%, not up to the 4.7% I have been quoting - Els (PSG) 4.7%, Hodes (Investec) 4.6%, Madikizela (Unisa) 4.4-4.6% with consensus at no change from May - so the live risk on my 4.5-5.0% band is now its floor, not its ceiling, and a 4.4% print scores that leg a miss. On the tape, the JSE All Share broke below 110k for the first time in this series, closing 109,570 on 17 July (-0.70%), Brent held $88.09 with Hormuz traffic still largely limited, platinum firmed to $1,609 and gold sat unchanged at $4,019.30; the SARB feed is still frozen on its 15 July close (repo 7.00%, prime 10.50%, ZAR 16.3712/$, NEER 85.42) against a market rand of 16.4825 on Friday.

Policy desk

The SARB should hike 25bp to 7.25% on 23 July, and the BER survey is now the cleanest justification on the table: when average expectations move roughly 80bp in a single quarter to 4.4% for 2026 - above the 3% point target and level with the Bank's own revised CPI forecast - the MPC is no longer managing a fuel spike, it is defending the target itself. The counter-case is stronger today than it was yesterday and I should say so: July's already-announced R1.96/litre petrol cut mechanically pulls headline back toward Els's 4.3% next month, so the Bank would be hiking into an inflation rate that is about to turn down, which is precisely why Els argues for a hold. I still favour acting, because a hike delivered while expectations are visibly de-anchoring is cheaper than one delivered after wage settlements have priced 4.4% in. On fiscal, the rules-based fuel-levy stabilisation band this ledger has pressed since 3 July would have absorbed both June's +143c/litre and July's -196c/litre swings without the repo rate having to arbitrate either.
